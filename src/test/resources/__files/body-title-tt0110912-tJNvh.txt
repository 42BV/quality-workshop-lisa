        <h2>Storyline</h2>
        
        <div class="inline canwrap" itemprop="description">
            <p>
Jules Winnfield (<a href="/name/nm0000168?ref_=tt_stry_pl">Samuel L. Jackson</a>) and Vincent Vega (<a href="/name/nm0000237?ref_=tt_stry_pl">John Travolta</a>) are two hit men who are out to retrieve a suitcase stolen from their employer, mob boss Marsellus Wallace (<a href="/name/nm0000609?ref_=tt_stry_pl">Ving Rhames</a>). Wallace has also asked Vincent to take his wife Mia (<a href="/name/nm0000235?ref_=tt_stry_pl">Uma Thurman</a>) out a few days later when Wallace himself will be out of town. Butch Coolidge (<a href="/name/nm0000246?ref_=tt_stry_pl">Bruce Willis</a>) is an aging boxer who is paid by Wallace to lose his fight. The lives of these seemingly unrelated people are woven together comprising of a series of funny, bizarre and uncalled-for incidents.                <em class="nobr">Written by

                    <span itemprop="reviewRating" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Rating">

                    <div>
                        <p itemprop="reviewBody">Quentin Tarantino&#x27;s Pulp Fiction is a terrific film. It also gets better with each viewing, especially if one of those happens to be on a big theatrical screen where all of the BIG compositions get bigger and more detailed. How much else is there to talk about it after all these years? It&#x27;s filled with dynamite, sudden and always interesting action, great and naturally clever dialogue, and memorable characters. Also, the acting is always something to behold as by turns straightforward, over the top, subtle, and just downright menacing and spot-on. The directing is one of the strongest that we&#x27;ve seen from Tarantino, as he makes his choices in pacing with shots in unconventional ways but never in a way that would be distracting. And writing, already noted, has been copied by many, and only equaled by a select few. <br /><br />The dance sequence. Samuel L. Jackson&#x27;s superlative monologuing. It has loyalty among low lifes, and many other odd characters that are all bad and not one is a villain or hero. And somehow even after years of parody and terrible rip-offs, it holds its own and- as one can say after seeing it at a midnight screening- holds its audience as much as it had the countless times before they saw it (or if they are, the first time). The first time you&#x27;re surprised, the second time you look for the clues or other ambiguity, and then the third time you laugh you head off. The fourth time... I&#x27;ll leave to you.</p>
